5. Bari Vecchia ▾
The old town of Bari, the Bari Vecchia, is one of those places where the architecture itself tells you everything. These limestone streets — some barely wide enough for two people to pass comfortably — have been here for a thousand years. The stone walls absorb the light differently at every hour of the day. Pale gold at noon, deep amber in the evening, and at night when the lamps come on, the shadows make the whole neighborhood feel suspended in time. Laundry still hangs from the upper balconies. Old women sit in doorways watching the street with the calm authority of people who have been doing exactly this for decades. That evening, the passeggiata — the Italian tradition of the evening walk — was in full effect. The main street filled with people dressed properly for the occasion, moving slowly, greeting each other, stopping to talk. This is not a performance for tourists. This is just how life runs here.

6. The Basilica of Saint Nicholas ▾
Most people when they hear the name Saint Nicholas think of one thing: red coat, white beard, December, presents. But there was an actual person behind it — a 4th century bishop from what is now southern Turkey, one of the most revered saints in Christian history. For the Eastern Orthodox world — Bulgaria, Greece, Russia, Serbia — Saint Nicholas is not a seasonal figure. He is the patron of sailors, of travelers, of the poor. And, as it happens, the patron saint of our own hometown, Burgas. The story of how Saint Nicholas ended up in Bari is remarkable. He died in the 4th century in Myra, in what is now Turkey. In 1087, a group of sailors from Bari sailed to Myra and brought his relics back to Puglia. The city built the Basilica specifically to hold them. For over 900 years, pilgrims have been making their way here from across the Christian world. The Russian connection is significant. For centuries, Russian Orthodox pilgrims traveled to Bari to pray at the tomb of Saint Nicholas. There is a copper plaque near the entrance acknowledging this. And in front of the church, in one corner, stands a statue of the saint. We looked at the inscription beneath it and stopped. The name was written in Cyrillic — our own alphabet, carved into stone in a medieval Italian church courtyard. One of those small moments of unexpected connection that travel sometimes gives you and that you never quite forget.

10. Matera: The Sassi ▾
Nothing prepares you for Matera. The approach is ordinary enough — you're driving through flat farmland. And then suddenly the land opens up and you're looking into a ravine of extraordinary depth. Built into the walls of that ravine, stacked in layers from the valley floor all the way to the ridge, are thousands of dwellings cut directly into the rock. Stone upon stone upon cave. A city growing out of the Earth itself, not placed on top of it. These are the Sassi di Matera. People have been living in these caves continuously for longer than almost any other inhabited site in the world — some estimates say 9,000 years. When you stand on the ridge looking down, you are looking at the longest continuously inhabited human settlement in Europe.

11. Matera: Modern History ▾
For most of the 20th century, the Sassi were considered a national embarrassment. In the 1950s, the Italian government forcibly relocated the remaining residents — tens of thousands of people — to new housing built outside the ravine. The caves were abandoned. The writer Carlo Levi called Matera a disgrace, a place of poverty so extreme it shamed the nation. Then slowly something shifted. Architects, historians, and artists arrived. The caves were cleaned, restored, converted. Hotels appeared inside former cave dwellings. Restaurants opened in spaces that had been homes a century before. In 1993, UNESCO named the Sassi a World Heritage Site. In 2019, Matera was European Capital of Culture. Mel Gibson chose Matera as the setting for Jerusalem in The Passion of the Christ. The opening sequence of the James Bond film No Time to Die was shot here. When you walk the streets and recognize the light, the angles, the stone — it clicks into place.

Rhetorical Questions for Engagement ▾
反問句用於吸引讀者注意力，不期望得到答案。影片中 'Look at a map of the world and find Bulgaria' 這種祈使句加問句的結構，引導觀眾參與思考。
Look at a map of the world and find Bulgaria. It's just kind of at a crossroads, close enough to the Middle East, to Asia, to all of Western Europe, and even to Africa.
使用祈使句 'Look at a map' 直接對觀眾說話，然後用 'It's just kind of at a crossroads' 解釋保加利亞的地理位置優勢。這種結構既直接又引人入勝。
Look at [noun] and find [specific item]. It's [description], [explanation of significance].
33 euros. 33 euros for a one-way flight to Italy.
重複 '33 euros' 兩次來強調這個令人驚訝的低價。這種重複修辭手法有效地突出了重點。
[Value/fact]. [Same value/fact] for [context/repetition].
使用反問句和重複來強調一個令人驚訝的事實：'A hotel room costs only $50 per night.'
'$50. Only $50 per night for a hotel room in the city center. Can you believe it?'
Comparative Structure: 'Unlike...' ▾
'Unlike' 用於引入對比。影片中用 'Unlike' 來比較他們在Seattle和Bulgaria的不同旅行體驗，有效地展示了地理位置的巨大差異如何影響旅行方式。
When we lived in Seattle on the West Coast of the United States, the world felt very far away. A flight to Hawaii, 6 hours. Mexico, roughly the same. Europe, most of a day with a connection. Here, it's different, completely different.
先描述在Seattle的情況（the world felt very far away），然後用 'Here, it's different, completely different' 引入對比。通過具體的飛行時間（6 hours, most of a day）來強化對比效果。
When [subject] lived in [place A], [description of experience A]. Here, it's [different/completely different]. [Specific examples].
用 'Unlike' 或 'Here, it's different' 結構來對比兩種旅行方式。
'When I traveled by bus, everything felt slow and tiring. Here, with the high-speed train, it's different, completely different. The trip takes only two hours.'
